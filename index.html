// why this project

I have spent 20 years designing and validating hardware for the automotive industry: high-pressure fuel systems, solenoid actuators, electric drives for BEV platforms, and more recently hydrogen injection systems for heavy-duty vehicles. It is the kind of engineering where physical things have to work under real loads, real temperatures, and real vibration, because the alternative is a field failure.

In the last few years I started seeing the industry shift. Robotics and artificial intelligence are no longer research topics - they are becoming part of how machines work and interact with the physical world. I wanted to understand that world from the inside, not just observe it from a distance. And I learn best by building things.

"Engineering has always felt like a form of magic to me. You start with an idea on paper and end up with something that moves, lifts, flies, or thinks. That gap between concept and reality is where the interesting work happens."

So I decided to build an outdoor autonomous rover from scratch. Not a kit. Not a demo project assembled from pre-packaged modules. A real machine, designed component by component, with proper calculations, explicit margins, and a documented rationale for every choice.

The goal is to learn, and to prove - at least to myself - that the engineering rigour built over years of automotive hardware development transfers well into the world of mobile robotics.

// the approach

This is a hobby project, so the budget is tight. Every component has to be justified.

3D printing is a central tool in this project, and a genuine passion. Not for aesthetic parts, but for structural and functional components: gears, housings, brackets, transmission elements. Engineering-grade filaments (PA6-CF, ASA, PETG-CF) make it possible to produce parts that would otherwise require machining or expensive off-the-shelf hardware. Designing for FDM, accounting for anisotropy, tolerances and moisture sensitivity, is itself a discipline worth documenting.

Everything in this journal is backed by numbers. Torque margins, current budgets, bearing loads, odometry resolution - if a component is selected, there is a calculation behind it. This is not a tutorial. It's an engineering record.

// where this can go

The first version of this rover - which I call Argo - is a 4WD skid-steering platform designed for outdoor terrain: grass, gravel, compacted earth. I went with wheels: mechanically simple, efficient, and reliable on the surfaces that matter for real-world outdoor use.

Once the base platform works, the possibilities are real: sentry patrol, crop monitoring, outdoor inspection. None of these are distant - they're natural next steps from a working platform.

For now, the goal is simpler: build something that moves correctly, measures its own position, and does not break. That's already a good start.
