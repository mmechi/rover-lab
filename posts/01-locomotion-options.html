#01 Analysis May 2025

Choosing the locomotion concept:
a personal Pugh matrix.

Before buying any motor, I spent time mapping the options. Mobile robotics has produced a surprisingly wide range of locomotion systems, each with clear strengths and real limitations. This post is my structured take on which one belongs in Argo v1 - and why.

The choice of locomotion system is arguably the most consequential decision in an outdoor mobile robot design. It determines the mechanical architecture, the motor count, the firmware complexity, the energy budget, and ultimately the kind of terrain the robot can realistically handle. Getting it right at concept phase avoids painful redesigns later. Getting it wrong means scrapping a lot of work.

Bruzzone and Quaglia (2012) published a rigorous taxonomy of ground mobile robot locomotion systems [1] that I used as a starting point. Their framework covers both research prototypes and commercial products. I adapted it to the specific constraints of this build.

// the design space

Locomotion systems for ground robots fall into three fundamental categories - wheeled (W), tracked (T), and legged (L) - plus four hybrid combinations. Each has a characteristic profile of strengths and weaknesses across mobility, efficiency, and complexity. Hybrids try to get the advantages of two approaches at once - the cost is always more build complexity.

Fig. 1 - Ground mobile robot categories, adapted from Bruzzone & Quaglia (2012), Mechanical Sciences, 3, 49-62. doi:10.5194/ms-3-49-2012 - CC BY 3.0. ARGO v1 highlighted in blue; tracked non-articulated (considered alternative) in amber.
W - Wheeled

Wheeled robots

High speed, high energy efficiency, simple mechanics. Limited obstacle crossing without articulated frames. The most common solution in structured and semi-structured environments. Includes differential, Ackermann, and skid-steering variants.

T - Tracked

Tracked robots

Large ground contact area gives good traction on soft and uneven terrain. Differential steering only. Medium energy efficiency due to track friction. More mechanically complex than wheeled but very well proven in field applications.

L - Legged

Legged robots

Maximum mobility in unstructured environments and step/stair climbing. High mechanical and control complexity. Low energy efficiency (especially static gait). Technology readiness lower than W and T for solo builders.

LW hybrid

Leg-wheel

Wheel + actuated swing arm. Normal terrain uses the wheels; for obstacles, the swing arm lifts the chassis over. This is the v2 concept I'm working toward: 4WD with one actuated swing arm per axle. One reason I'm going with 4WD now is that this architecture is a direct extension of it.

LT hybrid

Leg-track

Articulated tracks provide extended mobility over obstacles. Used in field robots.

WT hybrid

Wheel-track

Variable-geometry tracks or wheel-track switching. Versatile but mechanically complex. High build effort.

LWT hybrid

Leg-wheel-track

Maximum versatility. Very high complexity. Research-level platforms only. Not realistic for a first build.

// criteria for this build

The taxonomy above is domain-agnostic. To make it useful I filtered it through the specific constraints of this build. The mission - an outdoor rover on uneven terrain (grass, gravel, light mud) - already rules out several options. The additional constraints narrow it further:

Build feasibility: I'm building this alone, with FDM-printed structural parts and off-the-shelf electronics. No CNC, no custom castings, no actuators that require specialist sourcing. If the design requires it, it's not a design for this build.

Budget: deliberately tight. It forces prioritisation and keeps the project from growing into something that needs a commercial sponsor.

Outdoor terrain performance: grass, gravel, light mud. Not extreme terrain - no step climbing, no soft mud, no slopes above 20 degrees - but also not a flat indoor floor.

Control complexity: v1 firmware has to stay manageable for one developer who is learning as the build progresses. Every extra degree of freedom in the locomotion system is more code to write, test, and debug in the field.

Field fault tolerance: I'll test this outdoors, away from a workbench. I want a system that still moves when one motor fails, not one that stops completely and needs to be carried home.

Max speed: walking pace, not racing. The target is 3-4 km/h for outdoor inspection use - enough to be practical, low enough to keep the drivetrain sizing manageable.

Energy efficiency: battery weight matters at this scale. A less efficient locomotion system means more battery for the same range, which adds mass, cost, and bulk to an already constrained design.

Tech readiness / community: I'm learning as I build. A system with good open-source documentation, proven DIY builds, and an active community reduces the risk of getting stuck with no solution when it matters most.

// pugh matrix

The eight constraints above map into nine Pugh criteria. Outdoor terrain performance splits into two rows - uneven terrain traction and soft ground capability - because they test different physical mechanisms and both rank high for this mission. Budget becomes "Component cost". The three lower-priority criteria (max speed, energy efficiency, tech readiness) are in the matrix because they affect real-world usefulness even if they don't drive the decision.

The matrix below compares five locomotion concepts against the baseline - the 4WD skid-steering configuration I selected for Argo v1. Scores are relative: ++ significantly better, + better, = similar, - worse, -- significantly worse than the baseline. Weights follow directly from the criteria above (1 = low priority, 3 = high). Where my weighting aligns with external data, I've noted it: La Regina et al. [3], Table 7 scores tracked and wheeled platforms on the same terrain type and reaches consistent conclusions.

// reading the results

2WD differential scores -4. On six out of nine criteria it beats the baseline: fewer motors, simpler wiring, lower firmware complexity, lower cost. That adds up to +10 weighted points. The problem is the terrain criteria. With only two driven wheels and a passive front castor, I can't maintain heading when one driven wheel loses grip on wet grass or lifts off a bump - hence the double-minus on both uneven terrain traction and soft ground capability. Both carry weight 3, which translates to −12 points from just those two rows. The terrain penalty alone wipes out all the simplicity gains, producing a net of −4. On a flat indoor floor the score would flip; on this mission it doesn't.

The choice of 4WD over 2WD is not primarily about extra torque. Four independently driven wheels keep at least three in contact during typical outdoor bumps, preserving heading without active compensation. There's also a fault-tolerance argument: a 4WD that loses one motor still moves and steers; a 2WD that loses a driven wheel becomes uncontrollable. The extra motors are not for speed - they are insurance.

There's a third reason that doesn't show up directly in the Pugh: room to expand later. My v2 concept is a wheel + actuated swing arm - each axle gets a swing arm that can lift the chassis over obstacles while the wheels handle normal terrain. That is a natural next step from 4WD skid-steer: same motors, same drive control, one added actuator per axle. Starting with 2WD would mean redesigning the chassis from scratch.

Tracked configuration scores +1 - above the baseline, but only just. The terrain advantage is real and substantial: uneven terrain traction (++) and soft ground capability (++) at weight 3 each contribute +12 points combined. La Regina et al. [3], Table 7 backs this up: traction 5 vs 3, terrain adaptability 5 vs 2, ground pressure 5 vs 2, stability on slopes 5 vs 3 - all strongly in favour of tracks on non-cohesive uneven surfaces. What drags the score to +1 is build feasibility, which I rated −− (significantly worse), not just −. Designing a functional FDM-printed belt or link-chain track involves non-trivial choices: belt tension, idler geometry, debris-clearing clearances, joint wear under cyclic load. If I had rated it just −, the score would be +4. I chose −− on purpose.

Field fault tolerance is also scored − for tracked, even though Table 7 gives mechanical robustness equal scores (4/4 for both). The difference is what happens when a motor fails: a tracked platform with two motors loses steering completely if one fails. A 4WD with four motors can still maneuver with three. The hardware is equally robust; the system is not.

I didn't go with tracks - not because the terrain argument is wrong, but because at this dimensional scale, skid-steering a tracked platform requires enough torque to break static friction on both tracks simultaneously during a pivot turn. Wheeled skid-steer reverses individual wheels against a much smaller contact patch. For a v1 platform optimising for time-to-outdoor-test, the track is a future option - not this build.

Ackermann steering scores −12 and I moved on quickly. Car-like steering requires a mechanical steering mechanism - servo-driven front axle, kingpins, tie rods - all of which need precision and add failure modes. The control is also more demanding: Ackermann needs coordinated steering angle and wheel speed inputs, while skid-steer "is more straightforward, as it only requires wheel speed control" [3]. The tech readiness + comes from the fact that Ackermann is everywhere in wheeled vehicles - RC cars, Arduino tutorials, servo modules. The documentation is out there. The hard part is fine-tuning: steering geometry, Ackermann correction factors, servo backlash all need careful setup, and for a rover that needs to turn in place, I'd rather have zero-radius turning. This is not a race car.

Legged locomotion scores −16 and I was never seriously tempted. A quadruped capable of stable outdoor locomotion needs actuated joints with position and torque feedback, a gait planning algorithm, and structural members sized for impact loads with every footfall. The control problem alone - gait planning, ground contact estimation, posture correction - would double or triple the firmware scope. It's a future platform, not a starting point.

The Pugh matrix is a structured opinion, not a proof. The scores reflect judgement calls on relative importance, and different weights would produce a different result. What the matrix does well is make those calls explicit: instead of "I'll go with wheels because it feels right", there's a documented set of criteria, weights, and scores that I can challenge, revise, or hand to someone who disagrees.

Tracked scores +1 - barely above the baseline. That result is sensitive to exactly two ratings: the ++ on terrain (justified by Table 7) and the −− on build feasibility (my estimate of the FDM track design effort). Change either by one step and the result flips. That's the honest answer to "why not tracks?" - the terrain advantage is real. The build complexity rating is where I could be wrong.
