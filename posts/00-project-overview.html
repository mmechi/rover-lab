#00 Overview May 2025

Argo v1: the full picture
before the build starts.

Before going into individual design decisions, here I map out the whole project: what has been decided, what is being built, and where the roadmap leads. I'll update it as the build progresses.

Every engineering project benefits from a map. Not a Gantt chart - a real picture of how the pieces connect: mission drives design choices, design choices drive component selection, components drive firmware, the whole thing drives the validation plan. Without that picture, it's easy to build something that works in isolation but fails as a system.

This one is agile by design. Priorities can shift - a problem with one component can change the order of everything else, and that's fine as long as the overall structure stays visible. The map below is the reference. I'll update it as decisions are made, revised, or confirmed by test results.

Five functional planes, one root. The structure is not organized by discipline — not mechanical, electronic, software — but by what each part of the system actually does. Each plane cuts across all layers of the build.

Mission is the anchor. It defines the operating environment, the platform concept, the budget constraint, and the documentation commitment this journal represents. Every decision in the five planes gets checked against it. If a choice can't be traced back to the mission, it doesn't belong in the build.

Movement is everything that converts energy into physical motion: the structure that carries the load, the actuation chain from power source to wheel, and the control layer that translates a velocity command into motor behaviour. This is the foundation. Nothing else works without it.

Sensing is how the rover reads itself and its environment. Self-sensing — wheel rotation, inertial, power — tells the rover what its own body is doing. World-sensing — visual, ranging, positioning — tells it what the environment looks like. The split matters: self-sensing is available now; world-sensing is planned, and the architecture needs to accommodate it without being redesigned.

Thinking is where data becomes decisions. At the current stage, that means a reliable localization engine and a correct motion control loop. Later it means sensor fusion, path planning, and eventually full autonomous navigation. The compute hardware and software stack scale with the algorithms — the roadmap for this plane is the longest.

Communication is the channel between the rover and the operator. Uplink carries commands; downlink carries telemetry. At this stage the link is wired and the log is local. The architecture is designed so the transport layer can be replaced without touching anything else.

Mission Payload is the reason the rover exists. Surveillance, field sensing, inspection — these are the applications. None of this gets designed until Movement, Sensing, and Thinking are stable. What the first build phases teach me about the rover's real capabilities will define what payload is actually feasible.

Validation runs through all five planes, not after them. Each plane has its own bench tests and GO / NO-GO criteria. The logic is the same everywhere: confirm each subsystem in isolation before trusting it in integration. Without that, debugging becomes archaeology.

Each plane has at least one dedicated post. The ones already written are linked from the journal. The ones still open are placeholders for work that hasn't started yet — or that started but isn't ready to publish. That gap between the map and the journal is itself part of the documentation.
