#00 Overview May 2025

Argo v1: the full picture
before the build starts.

Before going deep into individual design decisions, this post maps out the entire project structure: what has been decided, what is being built, and where the roadmap leads. A living document that will be updated as the build progresses.

Every engineering project benefits from a map. Not a Gantt chart, but a real picture of how the pieces connect: mission driving design choices, design choices driving component selection, components driving firmware, the whole system driving the validation plan. Without that picture, it is easy to build something that works in isolation but fails as a system.

This one is agile by design. Priorities can shift - a procurement issue on one subsystem may change the sequencing of another, and that is fine as long as the overall structure stays visible. The map below is the reference. It will be updated as decisions are made, revised, or validated by test results.

# ARGO v1 ## Mission - Outdoor terrain navigation - 4WD skid-steering platform - Low budget, deliberate choice - Full engineering documentation ## Mechanical Design - Drivetrain sizing and margins - Planetary gear DC motors - Swing arm suspension - Engineering-grade FDM - Wheel and axle system ## Electronics - H-bridge motor drivers x4 - 24V battery power system - Hall-effect odometry x4 - IMU and sensors (planned) ## Firmware - Odometry engine (RPM, EMA) - Skid-steer motor control - Telemetry and data logging ## Validation - 15-phase bench test plan - GO / NO-GO criteria - Subsystem qualification ## Roadmap ### P1 - Mobility - IMU fusion + waypoint nav - RC manual override ### P2 - Perception - Camera + obstacle avoidance - ROS2 + SLAM ### P3 - Applications - Sentry / crop / inspection

Six branches, one root. Each one represents a work area that needs to close before the rover can be called functional. Here is what each of them means in practice.

Mission is not a formality. It defines the operating environment (outdoor terrain, not a flat floor), the locomotion concept (4WD skid-steering, chosen for simplicity and robustness over differential or Ackermann), the budget constraint (deliberately tight, to force prioritisation), and the documentation commitment that this journal represents. Every design decision downstream is justified against these four points. If a choice cannot be traced back to the mission, it does not belong in the build.

Mechanical Design and Electronics are the two hardware branches and they are tightly coupled. The drivetrain sizing determines the motor and driver requirements; the power budget determines the battery and wiring; the sensor placement is constrained by the mechanical architecture. Getting this coupling right on paper - before any part is printed or soldered - is the main purpose of the early posts in this journal.

Firmware is where hardware becomes behaviour. At this stage the firmware scope is deliberately narrow: reliable odometry, correct motor control, and structured telemetry. Nothing more. Keeping the firmware minimal in the first build phase is a conscious choice - it reduces the number of variables when something does not work, and it establishes a clean baseline that later autonomy layers can build on.

Validation is the branch most people skip in hobby projects, and the one I refuse to skip here. The plan is a structured sequence of bench tests, each with explicit GO / NO-GO criteria, covering every subsystem before integration. This is not about being rigorous for its own sake. It is about knowing, at each step, whether the system is ready to move to the next one. Without that, debugging becomes archaeology.

Roadmap is the honest answer to the question "where does this go?" The three priorities are ordered by dependency, not by ambition. P1 - Mobility closes the current build: IMU-fused odometry, waypoint navigation, RC override as a safety net. This is the minimum that makes the rover autonomous in any meaningful sense. P2 - Perception adds a camera and obstacle avoidance, and introduces ROS2 as the middleware layer. This is where the platform starts to interact with its environment rather than just traverse it. P3 - Applications is the open end: sentry patrol, crop monitoring, inspection tasks. Whether any of these gets built depends on what P1 and P2 teach about the platform's real capabilities and limits.

Each branch in the map has at least one dedicated post. The ones already written are linked from the journal. The ones still open are placeholders for work that has not started yet - or that started but is not ready to publish. That gap between the map and the journal is itself part of the documentation.
